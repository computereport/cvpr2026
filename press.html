CVPR 2026 Compute Report: Accepted Papers Use Twice the GPUs of Rejected Ones, H100s Remain a Minority

First large-scale compute analysis of a major computer vision conference reveals sharp hardware divides, geographic concentration, and a modest but consistent compute-acceptance link.

The Compute Report Initiative today released the first comprehensive analysis of computational resources reported by researchers submitting to CVPR 2026, one of the world's largest and most competitive computer vision conferences. Drawing on mandatory compute disclosure forms submitted by 16,034 papers across all decision outcomes, the report maps the hardware landscape of modern AI research with unprecedented breadth.

Submission Overview

CVPR 2026 received submissions from researchers across 69 countries. Of those with compute form data:

Total papers analyzed 16,034
Accepted (oral + poster) 3,985 (25.3% acceptance rate)
Accepted + Findings 5,448 (34.6% combined)
Rejected 6,162
Withdrawn 4,139

More Compute, Higher Acceptance: But Not the Whole Story

Accepted papers reported a median of 4 GPUs and 2,000 development hours, compared to just 2 GPUs and 1,518 hours for rejected papers, a 2× GPU advantage and 32% more development time. GPU memory tells a similar story: accepted papers reported a median of 48 GB versus 40 GB for rejections.

Accepted    Rejected
Median GPUs 4.0           2.0
Median GPU memory 48 GB        40 GB
Median development compute 2,000 hrs    1,518 hrs
Median dev/training multiplier 3.7×          3.6×
Median project duration 7.0 months  7.0 months

Notably, the dev/training multiplier (total development compute relative to final training compute) and project duration are nearly identical across outcomes, suggesting that research process efficiency, not just scale, distinguishes accepted work.

H100s Are the Exception, Not the Rule

Despite the narrative around frontier AI hardware, only 11.6% of papers reported using H100 or H200 GPUs. The majority of CVPR 2026 research ran on prosumer and workstation-class hardware:

Prosumer / Workstation (RTX 4090, A6000…) 34.6%
NVIDIA A100 28.4%
Mid-high (RTX 3090, V100…) 17.2%
H100 / H200 11.6%
Entry / Mid (RTX 3080, T4…) 3.2%
Other / unclassified 5.0%

On infrastructure, over half of accepted papers used institution-owned on-premises hardware (49.9%), followed by shared academic clusters (27.9%) and commercial cloud (19.2%). The high on-premises share underscores continued reliance on institutional compute rather than cloud providers.

Geographic Concentration: China and the Long Tail

China accounts for 53.3% of all submissions (8,552 papers), more than the rest of the top 10 countries combined. The United States follows at 13.6% (2,185 papers) and South Korea at 6.1% (972). Submissions span 69 countries, though the distribution is heavily concentrated.

China 8,552 papers, 25.0% acceptance rate
United States 2,185 papers, 27.1% acceptance rate
South Korea 972 papers, 25.7% acceptance rate
Hong Kong 394 papers, 33.2% acceptance rate
Singapore 381 papers, 27.3% acceptance rate
United Kingdom 350 papers, 26.0% acceptance rate
Germany 340 papers, 29.4% acceptance rate

Acceptance rates are broadly similar across the top submitting nations, ranging from 24–34%, suggesting that peer review outcomes are not systematically skewed by geography among high-volume countries.

About the Compute Report Initiative

The Compute Report Initiative is an independent effort to bring transparency to the computational resources underlying modern AI research. The full interactive dashboard (geographic maps, per-country breakdowns, and GPU tier analyses) is publicly available at computereport.github.io/cvpr2026.
